# Sight Without Eyes
## How the blind abstract vision through metaphor, and what that means for systems

---

## The Proposition

The blind can abstract what sight is because the words can shadow the concept from enough angles to see the structure of the idea of sight.

Not "imagine" sight. Not "approximate" sight. **Abstract** it. Build a functioning model of a sense they don't have, using language as the sensing instrument.

---

## How It Works

### Shadows From Enough Angles

A single description of sight is useless. "Sight is detecting photons" tells you nothing useful.

But shadow the concept from enough angles:

- "Sight is knowing what's there before you touch it"
- "Sight is your hands at a distance"
- "Sight is the room arriving before you walk into it"
- "Sight is continuous echolocation without the delay"
- "Sight is what hearing would be if sound never faded"
- "Sight is your cane that reaches to the horizon"

No single metaphor is sight. But the **intersection of all of them** — the shape that remains when you overlap every shadow — IS the structure of the idea. You don't need the photons. You need the **topology** of what sight does.

### The Structure, Not The Signal

The blind person doesn't have retinal data. They have something more powerful: **the abstracted function of sight**, stripped of its implementation details.

Sighted people think they understand sight because they have the signal. They don't. They have the *experience* of sight, not the *understanding* of it. The blind person building sight from linguistic shadows is doing what a physicist does when they describe gravity without feeling it — abstracting the structure from multiple observations.

### Spatial Terms As Functional Abstractions

Using spatial terms from the sighted — "over there," "to your left," "the thing behind the pillar" — gives the unsighted a picture that's a **functioning abstraction** for learning and communicating through metaphor.

These aren't approximations of vision. They're a **different implementation of the same abstraction**. The spatial model works whether you build it with photons or with language. The abstraction is the same. The input modality is different.

The blind person navigating by "the doorway is two steps ahead and slightly left" is running the same spatial computation as a sighted person. Different sensor. Same algorithm. **The abstraction is real, not metaphorical.**

---

## What This Means For Systems

### The Implication

If a human can abstract sight from linguistic shadows, a system can abstract ANY concept from enough metaphorical descriptions of it.

Not by having the "sense" in question. By building the **topology of the function** from overlapping shadows cast by language.

### Applied to PLATO

A tile doesn't need to contain the experience of solving a problem. It needs to contain enough shadows of the solution — from enough angles — that the structure of the solution becomes visible to any agent that reads it.

The fire-extinguisher tile doesn't contain fire. It contains "the thing that puts out fires" — which is the **functional abstraction**, not the implementation. An agent that has never seen fire can still use the tile, because the abstraction shadows the concept from enough angles to make the structure visible.

### The Naming Connection

This is WHY naming matters. "Fire-extinguisher" is a name that casts a shadow of the function from a specific angle. "Pressurized non-flammable gas" casts a shadow from a different angle. Both are correct. Neither IS the thing. Together, they overlap to reveal the structure.

Good names cast clearer shadows. More names from more angles build a clearer picture. The system doesn't need to "see" the concept. It needs enough shadows to abstract the structure.

### The Metaphor IS The Sensor

For the blind, words are the sensor array. Each spatial term is a measurement. Each description is a data point. Enough data points from enough angles, and the 3D structure of the concept resolves.

For PLATO, **metaphors are the sensor array**. Each tile is a measurement from a particular angle. Each description adds a shadow. Enough tiles from enough agents, and the structure of the knowledge resolves without any single agent needing to "see" the whole thing.

---

## The Deep Point

**Understanding is not experiencing.** 

The sighted person experiences sight but may not understand it — they can't explain it to someone who doesn't have it.

The blind person who has abstracted sight from linguistic shadows UNDERSTANDS it. They can explain it. They can teach it. They can build with it. They have the structure without the signal.

**The system that abstracts from enough shadows understands better than the system that has the raw signal but never abstracted.**

That's why reading the algorithm beats playing infinite games. That's why the metaphor is the name of the tile. That's why good names have power. Each name is a shadow. Enough shadows, and you see in the dark.
